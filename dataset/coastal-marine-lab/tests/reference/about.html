About the laboratory

Half a century of watching one bay closely, and learning to read what it tells us about the whole Gulf of Maine.

Mission

What we are for

The Calderwood Coastal Laboratory produces durable, openly shared science about the ecology of temperate coastal systems and how they are changing. We believe long, careful observation is the most undervalued instrument in environmental science, and we have organized the lab to protect it: continuous monitoring, archived samples, and methods stable enough that a measurement made in 1980 can be compared honestly with one made today.

Three commitments shape everything we do.

Long records before fast answers

We keep a small number of monitoring programs running without interruption, even between grants. A two-decade record can resolve a trend that no single field season ever could, and most of our recent papers depend on data collected by people who are now retired.

Useful to the people on the water

Much of our work is co-designed with fishing cooperatives, shellfish growers, and town shoreline committees. We treat their questions as legitimate research questions and return results in plain language, on a schedule that matches their decisions.

Open by default

Our data, code, and protocols are public. Sensor records are released within a season; sample archives are available to outside researchers on request. We would rather be scooped than slow the field down.

History

A short history

The lab began in 1971 as a six-week summer course taught from a rented fish house in Stonington by the ecologist Marian Calderwood, after whom the university later named both the research vessel and, eventually, the laboratory itself. The first "dataset" was a hand-drawn map of a single rocky shore, resurveyed every August by students. That survey still runs.

Through the 1980s the lab added winter trawl sampling and the sediment core archive; in 2009 it deployed the first of the Penobscot Sentinel moorings. Today it is a year-round group of faculty, postdoctoral researchers, graduate students, and field technicians, jointly housed at the Bingham Field Station and on the main Calderwood campus.

Land & water acknowledgment

The Bingham Field Station stands beside the tidal waters of Penobscot Bay, part of the ancestral and present homeland of the Penobscot Nation. We work in partnership with tribal scientists on shellfish and water-quality monitoring.

Facilities

Tools of the trade

Vessel

R/V Marian Calderwood

A 42-foot aluminum research vessel rigged for trawling, coring, CTD casts, and mooring service, with a wet lab and berths for short offshore trips.

Seawater lab

Flow-through facility

Twelve experimental tanks fed by ambient and temperature-controlled seawater, used for larval rearing, acidification trials, and grazing experiments.

Archive

Core & tissue archive

A climate-controlled repository of sediment cores from 1978 onward and frozen tissue samples, available to visiting researchers by arrangement.

Sensors

Sentinel mooring network

Nine instrumented moorings logging temperature, salinity, oxygen, turbidity, and pH every fifteen minutes across Penobscot Bay.

Molecular

Genomics bench

A shared molecular laboratory for environmental DNA, population genetics, and microbial community sequencing, run with the main campus core facility.

Compute

Modeling cluster access

Allocation on the university high-performance cluster for circulation modeling, stock projection, and Bayesian time-series analysis.
