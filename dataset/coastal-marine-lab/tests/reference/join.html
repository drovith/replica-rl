Prospective graduate students

Do your graduate degree on the water

We advise doctoral and master's students through two Calderwood University programs: Marine Science and Ecology & Evolution, with a smaller track in Coastal Planning. Funded students join a working lab from their first semester — most are on the boat or in the marsh within their first month.

What we look for

  • A clear question you care about, more than a particular technique.
  • Comfort with fieldwork in cold, wet, early-morning conditions.
  • Quantitative curiosity — we will help you build the skills.
  • A collaborative temperament; this is a small, interdependent group.

How to apply

  1. Read the research programs and identify one or two potential advisors.
  2. Email that advisor a short note (half a page) describing your interests and attaching a CV. Do this before the application deadline, not after.
  3. Apply to the relevant graduate program through the university by December 15 for the following fall.

Fully funded positions include a stipend, tuition, and health coverage. We are committed to supporting applicants from backgrounds underrepresented in ocean science and will work with you on fee waivers.

Researchers & partners

Collaborate, visit, or use our data

We welcome postdoctoral fellows, sabbatical visitors, and collaborators who want to work with our long-term records or field infrastructure. The core archive, seawater facility, and Sentinel data are genuinely shared resources.

Ways to work with us

  • Postdoctoral fellowships — advertised positions and co-developed fellowship applications.
  • Visiting researchers — bench or berth space at Bingham, arranged a season ahead.
  • Data & samples — open datasets need no permission; physical samples are available by request.
  • Field campaigns — vessel time on the R/V Marian Calderwood for compatible projects.

To start, email a paragraph describing what you have in mind and the timeframe. We will route it to the right program lead.

Communities

Monitoring, together

Some of our most important work begins with a question from a harbormaster, a shellfish grower, or a tribal resource office. If you steward a piece of this coast and want to understand what is changing, we want to hear from you. We co-design monitoring that answers your questions and returns results in plain language, on a schedule that fits your decisions.

  • Town shoreline and flooding assessments
  • Water-quality and shellfish-area monitoring
  • Public talks and dockside data nights

Questions

Frequently asked

Do I need a marine biology degree to apply?

No. Our students come from biology, environmental science, statistics, engineering, geography, and the social sciences. A clear question and willingness to learn matter more than a specific major.

Can undergraduates work in the lab?

Yes — we host paid summer research assistants and supervise senior theses for Calderwood undergraduates. Watch for the spring call or email a program lead directly.

Is the data really free to use?

Yes. Published datasets are released under CC BY 4.0; you only need to cite them. Curated extracts and physical samples are available through our data coordinator.

Can I visit the field station?

Prospective students, collaborators, and press are welcome by appointment during the field season. See visiting details below.

Visit

Bingham Field Station

The station sits at the working waterfront in Stonington, at the southern tip of Deer Isle. The closest airport is Bangor (BGR), about ninety minutes by road. Let us know you are coming and we will arrange a tour and, tides permitting, time on the water.
