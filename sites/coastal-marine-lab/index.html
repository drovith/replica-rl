Our mission

A working laboratory for a coast under pressure.

The Gulf of Maine is warming faster than nearly any other ocean region on Earth. The Calderwood Coastal Laboratory exists to document that change rigorously, explain it plainly, and help the people who live and work along the shore prepare for it.

Founded in 1971 as a summer field course, the lab has grown into a year-round research group spanning marine ecology, fisheries science, sediment biogeochemistry, and coastal social science. We pair half a century of unbroken monitoring data with modern genomic, sensor, and modeling tools. Our results inform stock assessments, town shoreline plans, and the graduate scientists who will carry the work forward.

Research areas

Four questions guide the work.

Our programs are organized around durable questions rather than single grants, so that monitoring, experiment, and synthesis reinforce one another over decades.

Population & fisheries

Shifting stocks

As cold-water species retreat north, we track how lobster, herring, and groundfish redistribute — and what that means for fishing communities and management lines.

Estuarine ecology

The living margin

Salt marshes, eelgrass meadows, and mudflats buffer the coast and feed it. We measure how they gain, lose, and migrate as sea level rises.

Read the program
Biogeochemistry

Carbon & oxygen

We follow carbon, nitrogen, and dissolved oxygen through sediments and water columns to understand acidification and seasonal hypoxia in enclosed bays.

Featured project

The Penobscot Sentinel Network

Nine moored sensor stations and a monthly small-boat survey now form one of the most complete pictures of an embayment in the northwest Atlantic. Every fifteen minutes the array logs temperature, salinity, oxygen, turbidity, and pH from the surface to the seabed.

The Sentinel record has already detected earlier spring stratification, lengthening low-oxygen episodes in the upper bay, and a measurable northward creep of warm-water plankton. All data are released openly within a season of collection.

  • Continuous since 2009 — no gaps longer than nine days
  • Co-designed with three local fishing cooperatives
  • Feeds the state's seasonal hypoxia advisory
