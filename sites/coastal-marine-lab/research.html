Research programs & projects

Four long-running programs, a shared monitoring backbone, and a rotating set of funded projects that test specific questions within them.

Our research is built to last longer than any one grant. Each program below maintains its own continuous observations, contributes to shared infrastructure such as the Penobscot Sentinel Network, and supports student-led projects. Where programs overlap — and on this coast they always do — we treat the seam as the most interesting place to work.

Program 01

Shifting stocks & fisheries

As the Gulf of Maine warms, commercially and culturally important species are not simply declining — they are moving, changing size, and arriving on new schedules. This program combines our winter trawl survey, fishery-dependent data shared by local cooperatives, and population genetics to follow those shifts and to ask what they mean for the people whose livelihoods depend on them.

Recent work has documented a northeastward shift in the center of the lobster fishery, mismatches between herring spawning and the plankton blooms juveniles need, and the early arrival of warm-water predators such as black sea bass.

Active projects

  • Lobster on the move — settlement plates and genetics tracking where juvenile lobster recruit as bottom waters warm.
  • Herring phenology — pairing acoustic surveys with Sentinel data to test spawning–bloom mismatch.
  • Newcomers — an eDNA early-warning survey for range-expanding southern species.

Program 02

The living margin: estuarine ecology

Salt marshes, eelgrass meadows, and intertidal mudflats are the working tissue of the coast. They shelter young fish, filter runoff, store carbon, and absorb the energy of storms. They are also the habitats most exposed to sea-level rise and coastal development. This program measures how the living margin gains and loses ground, and whether it can migrate inland fast enough to keep up.

We maintain marsh surface-elevation tables, an annual eelgrass mapping program, and long-term mudflat invertebrate transects. Increasingly we ask not only how much habitat remains, but where it is going and what stands in its way.

Active projects

  • Marsh on the run — modeling landward marsh migration against parcel boundaries and roads.
  • Eelgrass recovery — testing transplant methods in bays where meadows collapsed in the 2010s.
  • Mudflat carbon — quantifying buried carbon across the intertidal gradient.

Program 03

Carbon, oxygen & biogeochemistry

What dissolves in seawater decides what can live in it. This program follows carbon, nitrogen, and dissolved oxygen through the bay's sediments and water column to understand two intertwined problems: coastal acidification, which threatens shell-forming species, and seasonal low-oxygen events in poorly flushed coves.

Using the Sentinel sensors, sediment incubations, and the core archive, we reconstruct how these conditions have changed over decades and forecast where the next hypoxic summer is most likely.

Active projects

  • Quiet coves — mapping which embayments are most vulnerable to summer hypoxia.
  • Shell budgets — measuring carbonate saturation where shellfish are farmed.
  • Buried history — reading nitrogen loading of the past century from dated cores.

Program 04

Coastal climate adaptation

Science only matters here if it reaches the dock and the town meeting. This cross-cutting program works directly with communities to turn our physical and biological findings into usable guidance: where to expect erosion, how working waterfronts might adapt, and which management choices keep options open as the climate continues to change.

It is deliberately interdisciplinary, pairing our ecologists with social scientists, and it treats local and Indigenous knowledge as data rather than anecdote.

Active projects

  • Working waterfront futures — scenario planning with three harbor towns.
  • Flood lines — translating sea-level projections into parcel-scale maps.
  • Knowledge keepers — structured interviews preserving long-term local observation.
